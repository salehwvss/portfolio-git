Phase

The Game: Phase is a space exploration and puzzle game that takes place on a space station that is floating throughout the galaxy. Unfortunately, the space station gets hit by a meteor shower, which damages part of the station and alters its trajectory directing the space station straight towards a deadly asteroid field. As the sole survivor on the station, the goal of the user is to go through a series of puzzles to reach the backup generator room and return the station back to its original trajectory. The user begins in a room, which acts as a start screen featuring three consoles: one containing information regarding the controls, one featuring information about the story world and the middle console contains a button allowing the user to “begin” the game.



Team: We developed the game in teams of 5.
Roles: We devided the project into levels. Each person was responsible for designing and programming a puzzle-based level. I proposed conveyor level idea, and designed and programmed it from start to finish.
My Role: I was responsible for the Conveyor Room. Coding/scripting, lighting, modeling, sound effects, interactions and ensuring the puzzle was running smoothly. I was also responsible for Generator Room's code.




















Command Room:

The Command Room is the initial room the user wakes up in. The automatic door in the Command Room has been affected due to the hit from the meteor - the user must figure out how to power up the door in order to open it. The user needs to locate a small energy source, which is located somewhere in the room and place it in a specific spot in order to open the door. The user needs to search the room for the power source, but can also take the time to explore the room if they wish. While exploring the room, the player will notice several things. They will see a television screen displaying an low battery warning as well as hear multiple warning sounds notifying the power is low. Once the energy source is slotted into the center console, the alarms and other alerts will stop beeping. More importantly, the door will unlock which allows the player to progress to the next level.

Video is the earlier version



Tile Room:

The user navigates from the Command Room to the Tile Room and Living Quarters. The user can explore the Living Quarters placed to add a touch of realism, such that people would actually live in the space station. Some objects here are grabbable, but not necessary to progress. The user will eventually notice the darkened hallway that leads towards the Tile Room. Before the Tile Room, there is a simple puzzle and console upon which the player must input a simple pattern to open the first door. This is to teach them how to understand and read the Tile Room. The Tile Room hosts the first puzzle: the user navigates through a hallway scattered with hints, and eventually enters a room and sees a four-sided console in the middle of the room. Each side of the console is made of up of 9 tiles. Each side is marked with a number (1 to 4). The user has to create the right pattern on each side of the console in order to power up the room and the surrounding area.

Video is the earlier version



Destroyed Hallway:

The next big challenge for the user is to navigate across a destroyed section of the space station. One entire section of the space station has been destroyed from the meteor hit - to get to the other side of the space station you need to activate the magnetic photon generator. Players will need to locate two energy packs located somewhere in the destroyed hallway and insert the energy packs to power the magnetic photon generator. Once powered, some of the debris will align itself creating a path for the user to navigate across destroyed section of the station and make it safely to the other side. The player is at risk of falling off, and they will die and respawn if they fall. Additionally, failing to activate the magnetic photon generator will make it impossible to pass the level. Several hints are provided to help players solve the problem. A warning video which informs players about the magnetic field detablization. The magnetic photon generator also has slot for 4 energy packs, two of which already have energy packs inserted. This is to let the player know where to place the energy packs.

Video is the earlier version



Conveyor Belt Room: (My level)

The conveyor room holds the final puzzle: the room features a series of conveyors with three blocks moving along them: a red block, a green block and a yellow block. The user needs to use three different consoles to change the direction of the conveyors and have all three blocks align on the correct places (tiles that match the colour of the blocks). Once the user aligns all three blocks in the right place - the door to the generator room opens up. If the user place one of the boxes in the correct place, but accidentally places another box in that area, the conveyor will reset and start to carry the two boxes together which makes the puzzle harder to solve because the user has to separate the boxes using the conveyors and consoles. After completing the conveyor puzzle, the user is able to enter the generator room: the final stage of our experience. The user presses the button to active the generator, which restores power back to the space station! After a short delay, the user is taken back to the menu screen.

Sketches




This sketch is used to conceptualize the 3-dimensional layout of the space station. A command center is in the centre between the rooms, which the user can use as a “home base” that they may always return to. The space station is formed with each module having a hallway to the center, as well as having hallways around the outside. This forms a ring, allowing the user to access adjacent rooms directly. The user will not always have to pass through the centre in order to go to a different room, allowing ease of access, and the ability to add more intricate movement restrictions, should we choose to pursue them in the future. For this early sketch, spheres are currently the shape of each module as well as the command center. ​

The first sketch depicts a long tunnel, aligned with pipes and panels. The player will use these corridors to navigate different section of the space station. The main purpose of the corridors is transportation. In some cases the corridors will be broken or damaged, forcing the player to travel in certain directions, or complete specific tasks to gain access.

The third sketch attempts to convey some aspect of the station. The station is made up of several segments and each piece of the station does not have to be identical or similar to other parts of the station. This sketch was an attempt to provide a different visual look to the station.

The top left sketches indicate what the space station would look like. The station is made up of 5 modules with the command module in the center. Each module is shaped like an egg where there are 2 separate floors for each module. The modules are connected via a cylindrical tube. The sketches show the thought process on what kinds of puzzles would be in the game. One such puzzle is the need to supply a console or power module with a power cell that has enough power inside. With enough power, the user could pull the handle to turn on the electrical systems. With the electrical system online, the door to another module would become unlocked. The sketch that shows the door indicates what this would look like. The door has two lights on each facing. These lights represent whether they are locked or not. If it is green, then it is unlocked, if red, then it is locked.

Project implementation process

We began the process by generating several ideas. We all finally agreed that we wanted to head in the direction of a space-based, puzzle VR experience. Initially, we had chosen the name PHASE based not only off of our initials, but also because our first idea was that the user would be “phasing” through time, they would see the before and after of the space station both intact and destroyed, and would have to prevent the space station from being destroyed. However we eventually decided to ditch the idea and instead had it such that it focuses on the after the space station has already been damaged, and the user has to instead escape. Since we decided to do a puzzle-based game, we split up the work by deciding on a number of puzzles we wanted to implement, and we had decided that we would each work on a puzzle separately. That way we were working in isolation and would be able to join our parts together as needed later on, in a modular way.



Components of the VR application (i.e. 3D assets, scripting, audio)

When creating our project, we utilized multiple components which when pieced together, created an experience that allows the player to be immersed in. The first component are 3D models and other 2D assets. Most of our model assets are downloaded from the asset store such as our walls, tables, decorations, and other environmental objects. However, there are some assets that we made ourselves using Maya, Photoshop, After Effects, Pro Tools, and other editing and design software. Notably debris, alarms, graphics for the screens, particles, and other minor models that are used in the background of the scenes were generated by us. Scripting-wise, all of our scripts are made by ourselves with the exception of the oculus VR framework which includes scripts for movement and grabbing. For the audio, we took most of our audio from freesounds.org such as the alarms, music, and other effects. However, there are some that we have made ourselves utilizing the Pro Tools audio software such as the generator sounds.
